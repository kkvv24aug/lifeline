#### For my constant,
#### from her disturbed lover

She was born between apology and hope,
a wish whispered wrong.
The second girl.
Not the boy they prayed for,
but a miracle they failed to protect.
They named her Laxmi,
and then taught her scarcity.

At four, her sister left for school in the hills.
She became her own guardian.
Sat under skies that never answered back,
counting clouds,
saving chocolates,
folding paper cards,
practicing affection in advance,
for her sister’s return,
learning devotion early
for people who were always leaving.

Her baba,
her truest friend,
her anchor,
listened without interruption.
With him she learned
that love could be gentle.
And when he left,
the house forgot how to breathe.

She learned abuse
before she learned language.
Words sharp enough to bruise bones.
Names no child should grow into.
Morning didn’t begin with good morning,
it began with survival.
And she still woke up soft.

She has seen death
before she learned to spell it.
A brother born,
a brother gone.
The first lesson in how fragile
a heartbeat really is.
The air turned heavy,
and she learned to hold grief
without dropping her gentleness.

She was seven when she became a mother.
Holding her infant sister,
rocking her to sleep,
feeding her,
while her own knees
were still bruised from childhood,
that slipped quietly away.
Her hands, too small for duty,
yet somehow steady.

When the house turned violent,
she became the ceasefire.
When adults said “I’ll kill myself,”
she believed them.
She tiptoed through nights,
placing her palm on chests,
counting breaths like prayers,
deciding
again and again
that everyone else had to live first.

No wonder romance felt like a lie.
How do you trust love
when the love you grew up with
has always meant endurance,
and holding it over your head,
and violence,
and compromise,
and conditions?

And yet, she shone,
growing into brilliance.
Every corridor in school
knew her sacred name
Vaibhav Laxmi
the girl who won everything
and still apologized for existing.
Printed in newspapers,
infinitely etched in the memory of every teacher
and ones who ever met her eyes.

They called her perfect.
The obedient child.
Sanskari.
The quiet achiever.
Easy.
They never saw her swallow tears
so her mother could sleep,
her father could eat,
her sisters could feel safe,
and the world could stay convinced
that everything was fine.

Then came the pandemic.
And death stayed.
Her baba fading,
relatives counting land and money
before ashes cooled.
She cooked. Cleaned. Held the house.
Fifteen hours a day.
Learned resilience by force.
And when he died,
she didn’t just cry,
she hardened,
because grief had no space left.

Still, she showed up.
Still, she cooked.
Still, she smiled.
Because someone had to.
And because everyone else knew
she would.

Then her elder sister jumped.
Not slipped.
Jumped.
And she stood frozen in terror,
watching life almost leave
right in front of her eyes.
Blaming herself for everything.
Ambulance lights. Rain.
A night that never ended.
Her sister lived,
but the cost will echo forever.

She became everyone’s backbone.
And no one’s shelter,
priority.

Her body began screaming quietly.
Eight years of brutal periods.
Pain that bent her in half.
Still she studied.
Still she competed.
Still she loved.
“I’ll see a doctor later,” she said
because hospital bills frightened her
more than blood did.

Tell me
how do you not kneel
before that kind of woman?

She wanted to heal children.
A stethoscope dream,
born from loss.
She worked. She scored.
And still the world said,
not enough.
How do you explain to God
that someone like her
deserved more?

But she is way more than an unfair exam.
She is enoughness itself,
in every breath she takes.

So she joined a new world,
though scared,
still not over the status quo bias,
and her broken dream.

That’s when we met.
Two people shaped by chaos,
recognizing each other
without explanation.

She says I healed her.
But she’s the medicine,
and I’m just the bottle.
She healed me first.
I was just finally listening.

She taught me love
is not noise.
It is presence,
even when you’re tired,
even when you’re bleeding.
It is staying.
It is not leaving
when things get heavy.

Her patience taught me gentleness.
Her smile lit up my bleak world.
Her existence gave me purpose.

Yes, she gets tired.
Yes, she forgets her own softness.
Sometimes she breaks.
Her empathy collapses.
She fails to improve amidst chaos and chores.
The world keeps pouring
its pain into her hands,
and I—
I keep blaming her
again and again,
for being too quiet,
for being unproductive,
for not holding my pain
with enough care.
I didn’t see
that she’d run out of space
to carry more.
But I do now.
And yes,
I failed her.
I doubted her.
I let fear speak louder than care.
I pushed when I should have held.
I forgot how much she had already carried.

But I see her now.
Fully.
And I will not add to her weight again.
I'll never let her hate and blame herself,
because of how I and the world treated her.
I'll never let her believe she deserves the worst in the world, but the best.
I'll never let her kneel down just to ask someone, even me, to love her, ever again.
I’ll never let her fight alone again.
She IS my priority.

Because she,
my Vaibhav Laxmi,
is not fragile.
She is forged.
She is strength itself,
disguised as a cutu girl.
Soft stories what?
She is a storm with manners.
A wound that still offers warmth.
A goddess who bleeds and still believes in love
after everything love took from her. { .center .bold-glow .reveal }

And if there is heaven,
I’ll tell them I’ve already seen it.
In her.
In the way she lives,
loves,
endures,
selflessly.

She is the strongest woman.
Not because she never breaks,
but because she breaks,
and still chooses
##### love. { .reveal .delay-2 .text-pink }

### PS:

*I’m sorry.  
Truly.*

Yesterday, I let my fear and hurt speak instead of my love.  
I doubted you when you trusted me with the most painful parts of your life.  
I didn’t listen the way you deserved, and that must have hurt you.

Nothing you went through was your fault.  
Nothing you hid came from manipulation. 
It came from fear of losing me.  
And I hate that my reactions proved that fear right, even for a moment.

This poem isn’t to trap you, convince you, or pressure you.  
It’s just me saying I can never unlove you no matter what.
And I want to love you without making you fight anymore.

I don’t expect things to get normal instantly.  
I just want the chance to show it slowly and consistently, that I can be safe for you.
So you never have to hide things from me, ever.

*I love you.  
And I'll keep loving you till my final breathe, whenever it will be.*
