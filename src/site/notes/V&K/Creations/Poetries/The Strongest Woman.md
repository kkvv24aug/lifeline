#### For my lifeline,
#### from her devotee

She was born between apology and hope,
a wish whispered wrong.
The second girl,
not the boy they prayed for,
but a miracle they didn’t recognize yet,
the goddess laxmi herself.

At four, her sister left for school in the hills.
She became her own guardian.
She sat under skies that never spoke back,
counting clouds,
saving chocolates,
folding paper cards,
practicing affection in advance,
for her sister’s return,
learning to love people
who were always leaving.

Her grandfather,
her baba
was her truest friend,
her anchor,
her quiet universe.
The only one who never made her feel
too much, or too little.
With him she learned what listening 
without interruption meant,
and what silence cost.

She has seen death
before she learned to spell it.
A brother born,
a brother gone.
The first lesson in how fragile
a heartbeat really is.
The air turned heavy,
and she learned to hold grief
without dropping her gentleness.

She was seven when she became a mother.
Holding her infant sister,
rocking her to sleep,
feeding her,
while her own knees
were still bruised from childhood,
that slipped quietly away.
Her hands, too small for duty,
yet somehow steady.

When her parents fought,
she became the ceasefire.
When they said, “I’ll kill myself,”
she didn’t know it was a phrase.
She tiptoed at night
to check if they were breathing,
pressing her little palm on their chest,
checking the pulse,
like a prayer.

No wonder she never liked romance.
How could she believe in happy endings
when love had always meant
damage control?

And yet, she shone,
growing into brilliance.
Every corridor in school
knew her sacred name —
Vaibhav Laxmi —
the girl who won everything
and still apologized for existing.
Printed in newspapers,
infinitely etched in the memory of every teacher
and ones who ever met her eyes.

They called her perfect.
The obedient child.
The quiet achiever.
But no one saw her eat her tears
after holding everyone together
for another exhausting day,
so her mother could sleep,
her father could eat,
her little sister could calm down,
and the world could believe
that she was fine.

Then came the pandemic,
and with it, death again.
Her baba’s breath fading
while the world burned.
The rich bitch relatives counting inheritance,
before the ashes cooled.
While she cooked and cleaned,
handled home,
fifteen hours a day,
Learned recipes of resilience
and the bitter taste of betrayal.
And when he died,
she didn’t just cry,
she fossilized.

Still, she cooked.
Still, she smiled.
Because someone had to.
And she knew everyone else 
would just escape, delegating it upon her.

Then her elder sister fell,
and couldn’t walk for months.
She took her care,
checked on her dreams,
just like she used to,
at night,
if she's still on the floor, 
not on the fan.

She was everyone’s backbone,
and no one’s priority.

Her own body rebelled quietly.
Eight years of unbearable periods,
pain that clawed through her abdomen,
yet she’d still show up to school,
to class, to exams, to competitions, to love.
She said, I’ll go to a doctor later,
because hospital bills scare her
more than bleeding does.
How do you not fall to your knees
before that kind of woman?

She held the house together
when even walls wanted to collapse.
She became strength
for her mother,
hope for her sisters,
and still,
found time to dream.

Her dream had a stethoscope in it,
to heal what she could not as a child.
To become a pediatrician.
But fate played its cruel joke.
Even with outstanding marks,
the world said "not enough".
How do you explain to God
that someone like her
deserved more?

But she is way more than an unfair exam.
She is enoughness itself,
in every breath she takes.

So she joined a new world,
though scared,
still not over the status quo bias,
and her broken dream.
That’s when I found her.
Or maybe she found me.
Two stories with too much ache
to fit in one heart,
yet somehow,
we did.

She says I healed her.
But she’s the medicine,
and I’m just the bottle.
She healed me first.
She taught me how love
isn’t loud or cinematic,
it’s showing up,
it's presence,
even when you’re tired,
even when you’re bleeding.
Her patience taught me gentleness.
Her smile lit up my bleak world.
Her existence gave me purpose.

Yes, she gets tired.
Yes, she forgets her own softness.
Sometimes she breaks.
Her empathy collapses.
The world keeps pouring
its pain into her hands,
and I—
I once blamed her
for being too quiet,
for not holding my pain
with enough care.
I didn’t see
that she’d run out of space
to carry more.
But I do now.
And I’ll never forget again.

So yes, I’ve hurt her,
when I should’ve held her instead.
But I see her now.
Truly see her.
And I swear to every god listening,
I’ll never let her fight alone again.
She IS my priority.

Because she,
my Vaibhav Laxmi,
is strength itself,
disguised as a girl
who still believes in love
after everything love took from her.
Soft stories what?
She is a storm with manners,
a wound that still offers warmth,
a goddess who bleeds and still believes.

And if there is heaven,
I’ll tell them I’ve already seen it.
In her.
In the way she lives,
loves,
endures.

She is the strongest woman.
Not because she never breaks,
but because she breaks,
and still keeps choosing 
##### love.
