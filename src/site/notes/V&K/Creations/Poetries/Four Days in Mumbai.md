I want to write this down
before life normalizes it.

Before the photos become
just another folder in my gallery.
Before the feeling
turns into memory
and memory
turns into story.

Because right now
it still feels unreal.

Ten days ago
I boarded a train on Holi morning,
while the rest of the country
was busy throwing colors at each other.

And I left.

Not because I hate festivals.
But because sometimes
life paints its colors
in quieter ways.

I was carrying
a backpack,
a suitcase,
and a research paper
with a name longer than most conversations:

*End-to-End Evidentiary Integrity in Neuro-Forensic Systems.*

A small idea
about protecting the truth
inside the human mind.

Funny how life works.

We were trying to preserve evidence
inside brains
while creating memories
that would rewrite ours.

---

The train stopped
just before Dadar.

For two hours.

No movement.
No explanation.
Just the slow torture of waiting.

And somewhere on the other side
of that unmoving train
two people were waiting for me.

One who has known me
since class two.
who's here,
just for me,
enduring through post-fever weakness.

And one
who now knows my soul
better than anyone else alive,.

I told them to go to the temple
without me.

“I’m an atheist anyway,” I said.

They refused.

Apparently
friendship
and love
sometimes believe in miracles
even when you don’t.

So I did the most reasonable thing possible.

I jumped off a halted train
in Mumbai.

Almost got hit by another one.

And that’s when the city
introduced itself.

---

Local trains
are not transportation.

They are
compressed humanity.

Thousands of lives
pushed into metal compartments
moving at terrifying speed.

People cursed my suitcase.

Rightfully so.

Someone asked
if I was new here.

I said
“I’m just here for a conference.”

And suddenly
the same crowd
that was annoyed with me
became my guide.

They told me when to get down.

They laughed and said
“You won’t miss Dadar.
The crowd will throw you out itself.”

They were right.

And somewhere in that chaos
I realized something.

Every person in that train
was doing this
every single day
just to put food on their table.

Sweating.
Running.
Enduring.

Life is a rat race
for most of the world.

And escaping it
often takes generations.

Humbling thought
for someone who was worried
about missing a temple.

---

Then suddenly
I saw them.

Two familiar faces
in an unfamiliar city.

And we laughed
like children
who had accidentally
broken into adulthood.

We were in Mumbai.

On our own money.

On our own decisions.

For our own work.

That realization alone
felt like an achievement.

---

We bathed
in a railway waiting room.

Fashion advice
came from my best friend
who studies design.

Confidence
came from the girl
who studies my heart.

And suddenly
three small-town kids
were ready to explore
one of the biggest cities on earth.

Colaba.

Marine Drive.

Gateway of India.

The Taj Hotel.

Naturals ice cream.

The sea.

Ah, the sea.

Sitting there
looking at something
that vast
you realize
how small you are.

And strangely
how beautiful that smallness feels.

Because every wave
looked like a reminder:

You don’t need to be infinite
to matter.

---

Local trains again.

Buildings passing by.

Thousands of windows.

Each window
a family.

Each family
a story.

Each story
a universe.

There’s a word for that feeling.

*Sonder.*

The realization
that every stranger around you
has a life
as complex as yours.

Travel doesn’t just show you places.

It humbles you.

At night,
we waved my bestie goodbye.
It was tough.
Seeing him go through so much in scorching summer,
guiding us as an experienced Mumbaiker,
just so I can have good memories.

How the fuck I get the best people in my life?
Eternally grateful.

She and I reached our hotel.
Alone in a locked room,
with freedom to make love.
And we did.
As much as we could.
Like there's no tomorrow.

---

Day two.

Conference day.

Ministers.

Professors.

Researchers.

People who had spent decades
thinking about crime
justice
and the human mind.

And there we were.

Two students
with a strange idea
about cryptography
protecting psychological evidence.

Our presentation,
preponed to the first day,
was the last one of the day.

Lack of preparation.
Fear of judgement.
Pressure from the amazing presentations before us.
And I fumbled.

A little.

Actually
more than I’d like to admit.

One reviewer
looked unimpressed.

And in that moment
my brain started writing
a tragedy.

Until something strange happened.

People came to talk.

They asked questions.

They appreciated the work.

One said
it was the most unique paper there.

Another suggested
expanding it into neurological treatment systems.

Someone else said
the creator of BEOS
was their colleague.

Small world.

Huge relief.

Sometimes
your biggest critic
is just the voice inside your own head.

And sometimes
you need someone beside you
to remind you of that.

She did.

---

But life
never lets a story
be simple.

She got her periods
that was due for 5 days.

Pain.

Cramps.

Vomiting.

The kind of pain
that makes you feel useless
because love
cannot physically remove suffering.

I massaged her.

Gave ORS.

Argued about medicine.

Asked her to see a doctor.

She refused.

Because treatment
costs money.

And that moment
hurt more than anything.

Because the strongest people
often learn
to endure pain
instead of treating it.

So I promised something.

Not poetry.

Not philosophy.

Money.

Enough
to make sure she never has to choose
between health
and finances again.

We cried.

Together.

And strangely
that felt like the most intimate moment
of the entire trip.

Watched a movie to distract
till we feel asleep
in each other's warmth.

---

Day three
was quieter.

Gentler.

Messier.

Real love
isn’t cinematic.

It’s awkward.

Tender.

Learning each other’s bodies
and histories
with patience.

I bathed her gently.

And realized
that intimacy
is not just desire.

It’s care.

It’s trust.

It’s cleaning blood,
and vomit,
and potty,
without disgust.

It’s listening
while someone tells you
stories they’ve never told anyone.

---

That night
another friend
fed us dinner.

People
kept showing up.

Helping.

Guiding.

Feeding.

Reminding me
that the world
is kinder than the internet suggests.

---

Day four
came too fast.

Checkout.

Luggage.

A kind hotel staff member
who stored our bags,
and then helped loading it in the cab
and who cooked dal khichdi upon request that day
for her who needed comfort food.

Human kindness
appears in the smallest places.

A hotel kitchen.

A crowded train.

A random professor.

A friend with money.

A partner
who refuses to leave you alone
even when you tell them to.

---

Buildings bigger and valued
more than our entire net worth.

So many rich people in the world,
and we have to be one of them to survive.
No time for resting and wasting.

CSMT station.

Architecture older than our worries.

The city still rushing
while we stood still.

My train came first.

Her train
much later.

So drank coconut water and juice.

Got her a retiring room 
so her tired body could rest.

And we pretended goodbye
was easy.

It wasn’t.

A last kiss.

A wave.

Two trains
in opposite directions.

---

But the story
still wasn’t done.

She got sick.

Fever.

A 3 a.m. arrival
in a city that isn’t safe.

She asked someone for help
and was reminded
why we rarely ask for help.

Because favors
often come with ego.

We argued,
for not trusting me but him.

A little.

Then remembered
we’re on the same side.

And stood together again.

---

And somehow
through missed trains
period pain
academic anxiety
crowded locals
fumbles
vomit
architecture
friendship
research
and love

we did it.

We presented our paper.

We survived Mumbai.

We created memories
bigger than the conference itself.

---

And now
I’m back in my hostel room.

Looking at the photos.

Trying to convince myself
those four days
actually happened.

Because it all feels like
a dream.

Maybe it was.

Or maybe
this is what life looks like
when curiosity
friendship
ambition
and love
all decide to travel together.

And if that’s true

then I hope
the next train, 
even if the wrong one,
takes us somewhere even bigger,
at the right destination.

But even if it doesn’t

I’ll always remember
those four days
in Mumbai

when three small-town kids
briefly felt
## infinite.
